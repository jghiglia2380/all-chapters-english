Part A: Market Equilibrium Analysis
1. Equilibrium at $20,000—quantity demanded (300) equals quantity supplied (300). Market condition: EQUILIBRIUM (no shortage or surplus).
2. At $30,000, there's a surplus of 300 cars (sellers want 400, buyers want only 100). Sellers can't sell inventory, so they lower prices. Price falls toward equilibrium as unsold inventory accumulates.

Common Misconceptions
Misconception Clarification
"High prices in {{STATE_NAME}} are just because of greed." Prices reflect supply and demand balance. {{STATE_NAME}}'s home price of ${{STATE_MEDIAN_HOME_PRICE}} reflects its specific combination of demand factors (population growth: {{STATE_POPULATION_GROWTH}}, job growth: {{STATE_JOB_GROWTH}}) and supply factors (construction trends, land availability).
"Supply and demand only applies to goods, not services or labor." Supply and demand applies to EVERYTHING with a price—including wages ({{STATE_NAME}}'s {{STATE_UNEMPLOYMENT_RATE}}% unemployment reflects labor market balance), rent ({{STATE_NAME}}'s ${{STATE_MEDIAN_RENT}}/mo), interest rates, and stock prices.
"If prices are high, they'll stay high forever." High prices attract new supply (builders construct more housing, workers enter high-paying fields). Over time, increased supply puts downward pressure on prices. Markets self-correct—it's a question of how quickly.
"The government controls all prices." While regulations affect some prices (minimum wage, rent control), most prices are determined by millions of individual supply and demand decisions. Government can shift supply or demand curves but rarely overrides market forces long-term.
